Whatever conditions were consistently present when you won — the time of day, the pair, where price was, how the moving average was curving, how long you held, where you placed your stop — those become your pattern. Whether your approach has a name or not is irrelevant.

If you trade a completely unique method that nobody else uses and has never been written about, TradeShadow still learns it. Because it's reading your outcomes and your chart conditions — not consulting a dictionary of strategy names.

When you upload your TradingView charts, the AI reads what is on screen and describes your approach in plain language — your indicators, your levels, your patterns, your bias method — creating your strategy description from scratch, specific to you. Not from a template. Yours.

Before You Begin

What TradeShadow Needs from You

For the AI to build an accurate model of your trading style and calculate precise lot sizes, there are a few things that must be in place before — and during — the learning period. These aren't restrictions; they're what makes the system genuinely personal to you.

◎
Required

Every Trade Must Have a Defined SL & TP

This is the single most important requirement. TradeShadow learns your risk management style from your stop loss and take profit placement — how many pips you risk, where you place your targets relative to key levels, and what your typical R:R looks like. Trades without a defined SL or TP cannot be analysed and will be excluded from your profile. If you currently trade without stops, you will need to establish this habit before the learning period begins.

◈
Required

Consistent Risk Per Trade (0–5% of Account)

TradeShadow reads your historical position sizing to understand how much of your account you typically risk per trade. During the learning period, the algorithm will identify your average risk percentage and use this to calculate lot sizes on future alerts. You must risk within the 0–5% range per trade for the algorithm to function correctly — excessive position sizing outside this range will produce inaccurate lot size recommendations.

◉
Required

Minimum 1 Month of Active Trading History

The AI needs a statistically meaningful sample of trades to build your profile accurately. One month of regular trading — typically 20 or more completed trades — gives it enough data to identify patterns in your session timing, pair preferences, entry conditions, and trade duration. The more trades in the dataset, the more precise the win probability scoring and lot size calculation will be. Existing MT4/MT5 history can be uploaded to accelerate this.

◌
Recommended

Trade Your Strategy Consistently During Learning

The learning period works best when you are trading your actual strategy — not experimenting or taking trades outside your normal criteria. If your style changes significantly during the first month, re-analyse your history at any time to update your profile. TradeShadow adapts as you do, but the cleaner the data, the sharper the results.

⬡
Recommended

Keep Your Account Size Stable During Learning

Lot size calculations are based on a percentage of your account balance. Large deposits or withdrawals during the learning period can skew the risk percentage analysis. Where possible, allow the learning month to run on a stable account balance so the algorithm calibrates accurately to your real trading conditions.

Your Personal Match Rating

The Shadow
Score.

Every setup TradeShadow surfaces comes with a Shadow Score — a percentage that tells you exactly how closely the current market conditions match your own historical trading criteria. Not a generic probability. Not a market sentiment rating. A direct comparison between what the market is doing right now and what you have historically entered on.

The Shadow Score is calculated from your own trade history. If price is at an area of interest you typically trade, in your session window, on a pair you favour, with the SMA alignment you require — the score is high. If any of those conditions are missing, the score drops. It's your strategy, reflected back to you as a number.

Only alerts that meet your plan's minimum Shadow Score threshold are sent. On Core, that's 80% and above. The score is not a recommendation — it is information. You still decide.

Chapter One

Thinking in Probabilities

One of the most profound shifts a trader can make is moving from thinking in terms of certainties to thinking in terms of probabilities. This concept sits at the core of Mark Douglas's landmark work, Trading in the Zone — perhaps the single most important book ever written about the psychology of trading.

Douglas observed that most traders approach the market as though each trade has a predictable, knowable outcome. They become emotionally invested in being right. When a trade fails, it doesn't feel like a probability playing out — it feels like a personal failure, a mistake, something that shouldn't have happened. This creates a cycle of fear, hesitation, and inconsistency that destroys more trading accounts than any bad strategy ever could.

"The best traders have developed an edge, and they trust that edge implicitly. They don't need to know what the next trade will do. They know their edge will produce results over a series of trades."

Mark Douglas — Trading in the Zone

The shift Douglas describes is deceptively simple: accept that any individual trade can go either way. What matters is not this trade — it's your edge across hundreds of trades. A strategy with a 65% win rate will produce losing streaks. That is not failure. That is the natural distribution of a probabilistic system working exactly as it should.

The traders who struggle most are those who cannot separate the outcome of a single trade from the quality of their decision. A perfectly executed trade that loses money is not a bad trade. A poorly executed trade that makes money is not a good trade. The quality of the decision and the outcome of the trade are two entirely separate things.

TradeShadow Perspective

This is exactly why the win probability feature shows you a percentage based on historical similarities — not to guarantee an outcome, but to remind you that you're operating within a probability distribution. A 68% probability setup will lose roughly 32% of the time. That is not a failure of the tool or your strategy. It is the system working correctly.

Chapter Two

The Four Trading Fears

Douglas identified four core psychological fears that cause traders to act irrationally — entering too late, exiting too early, holding too long, or avoiding trades entirely. Understanding these fears is the first step to overcoming them.

Fear 01

The Fear of Being Wrong

The ego equates a losing trade with being wrong as a person. This causes traders to move stop losses, add to losing positions, and stay in trades well past their original plan — all to avoid acknowledging a loss. The market has no opinion of you. A loss is simply a loss.

Fear 02

The Fear of Losing Money

Distinct from fear of being wrong, this fear causes premature exits and under-sized positions. Traders who fear losing money take profits far too early, cutting winners that would have reached their target. Ironically, this fear of losing creates worse overall returns than accepting losses cleanly.

Fear 03

The Fear of Missing Out

FOMO causes traders to chase moves already in progress, enter without their setup being present, and abandon their criteria entirely because they watched a trade they didn't take work. This is among the most destructive forces in retail trading and is amplified by social media.

Fear 04

The Fear of Leaving Money on the Table

The inverse of fear of losing — the inability to take profit at the target because it "might go further." This causes traders to turn winning trades into breakeven or losing ones, and creates a permanent dissatisfaction with every outcome regardless of result.

These four fears are not weaknesses unique to new traders. They are hardwired human responses to uncertainty and financial risk. The difference between struggling traders and consistent ones is not the absence of these feelings — it is a practiced ability to act according to their process regardless of how they feel in the moment.

"Until you genuinely accept the risk, you will experience the pain of the fear of being wrong, the fear of losing money, the fear of missing out, and the fear of leaving money on the table."

Mark Douglas — Trading in the Zone
Chapter Three

Lessons from the Market Wizards

Jack Schwager's Market Wizards series — interviews with some of the most successful traders in history — revealed a striking pattern. Across wildly different styles, time frames, and markets, the same themes emerged again and again. The psychological thread was more consistent than any technical approach.

"The most important thing is money management, money management, money management. Anyone who is successful will tell you the same thing."

Paul Tudor Jones — Market Wizards

Cut your losses and let your winners run. This advice sounds almost offensively simple. Yet Schwager found that traders who struggled most consistently did the exact opposite — they let losses run while cutting winners short. The emotional logic is understandable: taking a small profit feels good and safe; holding a loss feels like it might still come back. The mathematical reality is that this approach slowly destroys accounts regardless of win rate.

"I don't think trading strategies are as critical as money management and psychology. The key is not losing more than you can afford to lose."

Ed Seykota — Market Wizards

Every great trader has found their own style. Michael Marcus, one of the traders profiled in Market Wizards, built a fortune by developing a methodology that was uniquely his — one that suited his personality, his risk tolerance, and his way of reading the market. He explicitly discouraged copying other traders' styles, because without the deep belief and understanding that comes from developing your own approach, you will abandon the method the moment it draws down.

The common traits Schwager found in every elite trader:

Absolute Discipline

Every elite trader had a set of rules they followed without exception. Not most of the time — every time. Discipline was not about willpower in the moment. It was about having committed to a process before emotion entered the picture.

Acceptance of Uncertainty

None of the traders in Market Wizards believed they could predict the market. They all acted on probabilities, managed risk tightly, and understood that being wrong 40% of the time was entirely consistent with extraordinary performance.

A Defined Edge

Every trader could articulate — precisely — what their edge was. Not "I trade breakouts" but a specific, measurable condition that they had observed to produce positive expectancy over a statistically significant number of trades.

Emotional Detachment from Outcomes

The best traders treated winning and losing trades with the same level of emotional response: minimal. They had learned to evaluate decisions, not outcomes. This is one of the hardest psychological shifts in trading, and arguably the most valuable.

Chapter Four

The True Nature of the Market

The forex market is the largest, most liquid financial market in the world — with over $7 trillion traded daily. It operates 24 hours a day, five days a week, across every timezone. It is shaped by central bank policy, geopolitical events, economic data, institutional positioning, retail flow, and pure sentiment. No single person, algorithm, or institution controls it.

This is both the source of its opportunity and the source of most traders' psychological difficulty. The market is not a system that rewards effort, intelligence, or analysis in any direct way. It rewards the ability to identify and exploit a repeatable edge, manage risk precisely, and execute consistently over a large number of trades — regardless of what any individual trade does.

A Crucial Distinction

The market doesn't know you exist. It has no opinion of your analysis, your effort, or your confidence. A trade failing is not the market "going against you." It is probability playing out. Understanding this removes enormous psychological weight from the trading process.

Price moves because of the aggregate decisions of every participant — from hedge funds moving billions to retail traders making decisions on a phone. At any given moment, the market reflects all available information plus the emotional state of every participant. This is why technical analysis works — not because charts predict the future, but because human psychology is remarkably consistent. The same patterns of fear, greed, hesitation and momentum appear again and again across different instruments and time periods.

Volatility is not your enemy — it is the source of opportunity. But volatile markets require adjusted expectations. A pair moving 200 pips in a session requires wider stops than normal, or smaller position sizes. The structure is the same; the parameters change. Many traders are destroyed not by bad strategies but by failing to adapt their position sizing to market conditions.

Chapter Five

Discipline Is a System, Not a Feeling

One of the most persistent myths in trading is that disciplined traders simply feel more disciplined. That they have better willpower, stronger character, or less emotion. This is categorically false. Every trader — from retail beginners to the legends profiled in Market Wizards — experiences doubt, fear, greed, and the pull of impulsive decisions.

The difference is that consistent traders have built systems that remove the decision from the emotional moment. They define their criteria before they are watching a live chart. They set their risk before they are in a trade. They establish their exit before they have money on the line. When the moment comes, they are not deciding — they are executing a plan they made when they were calm.

"Throughout my financial career, I have continually witnessed examples of other people that I have known being ruined by a failure to respect risk. If you don't take a hard look at risk, it will take you."

Larry Hite — Market Wizards

This is why trading journals are not optional for serious traders — they are the foundation of improvement. Without reviewing your decisions and your outcomes separately, you cannot identify where your process is sound and where emotion is leaking in. The traders profiled in Market Wizards universally tracked their performance in granular detail.

Consistency is not about winning every trade. It is about executing your process identically on every trade — whether the last five were winners or losers. The moment you start trading larger because you're on a hot streak, or avoiding setups because you've just had three losses, you have broken the system. You are no longer trading your edge. You are trading your emotions.

The Hard Truth

Most retail traders lose not because they have bad strategies — they lose because they cannot execute their strategy consistently under emotional pressure. A mechanical tool that reflects your own historical criteria back at you, without adding opinion or emotion, addresses the execution layer of this problem directly.

Chapter Six

Where TradeShadow Fits In

TradeShadow was built with these psychological principles explicitly in mind. The core problem it solves is not technical — it is psychological. Traders who have a working edge still miss setups, chase setups, and make inconsistent decisions. Not because their strategy is wrong, but because of the four forces that Douglas and the Market Wizards consistently describe.

Eliminates FOMO

When you know TradeShadow is watching your selected markets continuously, the fear of missing a move is significantly reduced. You don't need to stare at five charts simultaneously or check your phone every ten minutes. The setup will find you.

Removes Outside Noise

TradeShadow shows you only setups that match your own historical criteria. It doesn't add opinions from other traders, analysts, or indicators you don't use. This directly reduces the cognitive load that leads to second-guessing and hesitation.

Reinforces Your Edge

By surfacing setups that match what has historically worked for you, TradeShadow strengthens your belief in your own methodology. Confidence built on data from your own history is far more durable than confidence borrowed from someone else.

Provides Probabilistic Context

The win probability percentage does what Douglas recommends — it frames each trade as a data point in a series, not an isolated event. Seeing "74% based on 18 similar trades" anchors your thinking in probabilities rather than certainty or hope.

TradeShadow cannot give you discipline. It cannot prevent you from moving your stop loss or doubling your position size after a losing streak. The psychological work described in Trading in the Zone and Market Wizards is ultimately internal — it requires reflection, journaling, and deliberate practice over time.

What it can do is reduce the environmental pressures that trigger irrational decisions. Less chart-watching means less emotional exposure. Fewer conflicting signals means less doubt. A clear, data-based probability means less hope and fear. These reductions matter more than they might seem.

Connecting TradeShadow to your trading account takes under five minutes. Here's exactly what happens at each step — including what TradeShadow can and cannot access.

Choose your broker from our list and select whether you're on MetaTrader 4 or MetaTrader 5. If your broker isn't listed, you can enter their server details manually — any broker that provides MT4 or MT5 access is compatible.

Your broker provides two passwords for your MT4/5 account: a master (trading) password and an investor (read-only) password. TradeShadow only ever requires the investor password. 3

TradeShadow Reads Your History

Once connected, TradeShadow reads your closed trade history — every position you've opened and closed, along with the time, pair, direction, size, entry, exit, and result. The AI starts building your style profile immediately from historical data. Over the first month of active trading, it refines this profile with new trades, improving its understanding of your current strategy rather than just past patterns. You can trigger a manual re-analysis at any time from your dashboard.

5

Scanner Goes Live

Once your profile is ready, the scanner activates across your chosen pairs. Alerts are delivered via push notification and email the moment a matching setup is detected — with entry price, stop loss, take profit, and win probability already calculated.

Core philosophy
"I do not take trades. I wait for trades to come to me. If the chart has not told me a complete story by the time I have ticked every box, there is no trade — there is only patience."
Every strategy below describes a specific market behaviour — places where institutional order flow becomes visible to a retail screen. The setups are not about chart patterns; they are about reading the activity that creates the chart patterns. The rules attached to each strategy are the boundaries within which I can identify that behaviour with high enough confidence to risk capital. Outside those boundaries, the setup may look the same but does not behave the same.
Why this method works
Institutions leave footprints
Large orders cannot be filled silently. Whenever a bank or fund needs liquidity to enter or exit a position, that activity prints as wicks, gaps, and sharp expansions. Every strategy I trade is a way to read those footprints.
Retail predictability is the edge
Retail traders cluster their stops in the same obvious places — above swing highs, below swing lows, beyond round numbers. Institutional desks know exactly where that liquidity sits and routinely sweep it before reversing.
Confluence over conviction
A single signal is noise. Two are coincidence. Three or more aligned signals in the same place is structural — that is when the probability shifts. I only commit when at least three independent factors converge on the same level.
Operating principles
01

The urge to take a trade is the surest signal that the trade is not ready. Markets reward the patient and punish the active. A missed move costs nothing; a forced entry costs capital and discipline simultaneously.

The strategies I trade
Strategy 01
Area of Interest (AOI)
Structural reaction zones from the higher timeframe
H4 / D1 origin Reversal
An Area of Interest is a higher-timeframe zone where price has previously reacted with significant force — usually the origin of a sharp expansion, the high or low of a clean consolidation, or a recently broken support that has flipped to resistance (or vice versa). It marks a price level where institutional orders are likely to sit again because they have demonstrably sat there before.
Market mechanic Large institutional positions cannot be filled in a single order without moving price against themselves. Desks therefore split fills across multiple visits to the same level. The first reaction is the cleanest because it reflects fresh, unfilled order flow. Each subsequent visit consumes more of that latent liquidity until the level no longer holds — the institutions that defended it the first time have already gotten their fills.
Execution rules
  • Mark the zone exclusively on H4 or D1. AOIs are higher-timeframe phenomena; anything plotted below H1 is noise dressed as structure.
  • Wait for price to arrive. Never enter on the way to the zone — only after price has traded inside it and shown a reaction.
  • Drop to M15 or M5 for the entry trigger: a clean rejection wick, a break of micro structure in the opposing direction, or a small flag forming away from the zone.
  • Stop loss 10–15 pips beyond the zone's far edge. Take profit at the next opposing AOI or a minimum 1:3 reward-to-risk — whichever is closer.
Common mistakes I avoid: Marking AOIs on M30 or M15 (no statistical edge); entering before price has reached the zone (anticipation, not confirmation); taking the third or fourth touch of the same level (liquidity already consumed).
Strategy 02
Demand & Supply Zones
The consolidation that preceded an impulsive break
H1 / H4 origin Continuation
Where an AOI reads reaction, demand and supply zones read origin. I identify the last consolidation block before a sharp expansion candle and treat that block as the footprint of the orders that initiated the move. The expansion proves institutional commitment; the consolidation immediately before it shows precisely where those orders sat. When price returns to that block, the remaining unfilled portion of the original order flow is likely to defend it again.
Market mechanic Institutional desks accumulate or distribute over multiple hours within a tight range, then release the trade when sufficient size has been built. The resulting expansion candle is the visible discharge of that accumulation. The original consolidation block — the base — is the only place where the remaining limit orders from that desk can still be filled at the original price.
Execution rules
  • The zone must originate from an impulsive expansion that broke prior structure. No fakeouts, no slow drifts, no mid-range consolidations.
  • Prefer unmitigated zones (price has not returned since formation). Each subsequent test reduces the zone's edge by an estimated 40–60%.
  • Entry at the proximal line of the zone via limit order. Stop loss beyond the distal line. Size the position to keep risk fixed regardless of zone width.
  • Target the next opposing zone or a structural extreme. Minimum 1:2.5 R:R or the trade is rejected.
Common mistakes I avoid: Drawing zones around small wicks (true zones come from consolidation, not single candles); trading mitigated zones with the same conviction as unmitigated ones; entering at the distal line instead of the proximal line (worse R:R, same probability).
Strategy 03
Liquidity Sweeps
The deliberate raid on resting stop orders before reversal
M15 / H1 trigger Reversal
Every clean swing high accumulates resting orders above it — stop-losses from longs already positioned and buy-stops from breakout traders waiting to enter. That cluster is liquidity. To fill large orders without slipping their own price, institutional desks engineer brief pushes through these clusters, transact against the released liquidity, and then reverse. The result is the trademark sharp wick beyond an obvious level followed by an immediate move in the opposite direction.
Market mechanic A desk wanting to go short into a rally cannot simply sell at market — they need a counterparty for every contract. Buy-stops sitting above a swing high are guaranteed counterparties. Pushing price through that level triggers those buy-stops, immediately providing the buy-side flow the desk needs to fill its short. Once the orders are filled, the underlying directional pressure (selling) takes over and the wick reverses. The two most reliable sweep windows are the London open at 07:00 GMT and the New York open at 13:30 GMT — they happen with near-clockwork regularity.
Execution rules
  • Identify obvious retail liquidity: a clear swing high or low, an equal-highs/equal-lows pattern, or a round number that has held twice.
  • The sweep must be fast and rejected. A wick that pushes through and closes back inside within one candle is real. A close above the level is a break, not a sweep — do not fade it.
  • Wait for confirmation: a micro-structure break on M5 or M15 after the sweep, or a sharp reversal candle with above-average volume.
  • Stop loss above (or below) the sweep wick with 5–8 pips breathing room. These setups regularly produce 1:4+ R:R because the reversal target is typically the next zone — far away — while the stop is tight against the sweep extreme.
Common mistakes I avoid: Trading sweeps in the Asian session (insufficient volume to confirm institutional involvement); entering on the wick itself (false breakouts can extend); skipping the micro-structure confirmation.
Strategy 04
Inducements
The minor structural level placed to trap retail before the real move
M15 / H1 trigger Reversal
An inducement is a small swing high or low positioned just before a major structural level. Retail traders, scanning for obvious breakouts, will enter on the break of the minor level — assuming it leads to continuation. Instead, price sweeps the inducement, reverses, and runs back through the genuine higher-timeframe level in the opposite direction. The inducement is the bait; the structural level is where the real move begins.
Market mechanic Inducements work because they exploit the difference between what retail charts highlight and what institutional flow requires. A breakout trader entering above a minor swing high is providing buy-side liquidity at the exact moment a larger desk needs to absorb it to fill a short. The setup is most powerful when combined with RSI divergence on H4 — divergence shows that momentum was already weakening before the inducement was even touched, confirming that the breakout is structurally unsupported.
Execution rules
  • Identify the obvious retail trigger: most recent swing high/low on M15 or H1, a clean trendline break, or a round number directly preceding a major H4 level.
  • Wait for the sweep — price must trade through the inducement and reject immediately (single-candle wick is the ideal signature).
  • No entry on the inducement spike itself. Wait for a break of the micro-structure on the reversal side to confirm the trap has been sprung.
  • Stop loss beyond the sweep wick — typically 5 to 8 pips. These trades regularly produce 1:4 R:R or better because the stop is tight while the target is the genuine structural objective beyond.
Common mistakes I avoid: Confusing every fake-out for an inducement (true inducements sit directly in front of a higher-timeframe level); ignoring momentum context (without divergence or oversold/overbought signals, the setup loses its strongest filter).
Strategy 05
Fair Value Gaps (FVG)
The unfilled imbalance left by an impulsive three-candle move
H1 / H4 origin Continuation
A fair value gap is the price range left behind when three consecutive candles fail to overlap — typically caused by an impulsive move that prints faster than the market can fill it. The gap represents inefficient pricing: liquidity that wanted to transact in that range was unable to. Markets are reflexively drawn to fill these inefficiencies, which is why price tends to return to the FVG before continuing the underlying trend.
Market mechanic When a sudden release of institutional volume creates a sharp impulse, the move is too fast to be evenly matched by counterparty flow. The result is a "skipped" range — buyers paid through ask levels that had no seller commitment yet. Once the impulse cools, market makers and momentum traders are drawn back to that range to harvest the unfilled liquidity. The FVG retest is the most common pullback trigger in trend-following institutional methodology because it offers a structurally precise entry with measurable risk.
Execution rules
  • Identify the three-candle FVG: candles one and three must not overlap, with the middle candle showing the impulsive expansion.
  • Wait for price to return into the gap. Enter on rejection wick at the FVG mid-point or the 0.5 Fibonacci level of the gap range.
  • Strongest setups combine FVG retest with a bull or bear flag forming back into the gap — this is the "flag-into-gap" confluence with the highest historical hit rate.
  • Stop loss beyond the opposite edge of the FVG. Target the prior swing extreme or 1:3 R:R, whichever is closer.
Common mistakes I avoid: Trading every FVG (only the ones formed from a structural break matter); entering on the way into the gap (anticipation, not confirmation); trading FVGs against the higher-timeframe trend (continuation only).
Strategy 06
RSI Divergence
Momentum confirmation, never used in isolation
H1 / H4 filter Confluence
I do not trade divergence as a primary signal. It is the filter that confirms or rejects my other setups. Regular divergence — price prints a higher high while RSI prints a lower high — tells me that momentum is fading even as the chart still looks strong. Combined with a sweep into supply, the trade becomes structurally and momentum-aligned. Hidden divergence — price prints a lower high in a pullback while RSI prints a higher high — tells me the trend has more room to run, which keeps me from prematurely closing a runner.
Market mechanic RSI measures the average gain versus the average loss over the look-back period. When price reaches a new extreme but the magnitude of recent moves is shrinking, the underlying buying or selling pressure is weakening even as the visible chart pattern continues. Divergence is therefore a leading signal of exhaustion — it identifies the moment institutional commitment is fading, often before any visible reversal. The reason I never use it alone is that exhaustion can persist for many candles before reversal occurs. It tells me when something is likely; my other setups tell me where.
Execution rules
  • RSI period is always 14. I do not adjust the input. Tuning indicators after the fact is curve-fitting.
  • Regular divergence is the reversal confirmation overlaid on AOI rejections, sweeps, and inducements.
  • Hidden divergence is the continuation filter — it keeps me in trending trades and out of premature exits.
  • Never the primary signal. Always the third or fourth confluence layered onto a structural setup.
Common mistakes I avoid: Trading divergence as a standalone signal (high failure rate without a structural level); fading every divergence in a strong trend (markets stay in divergence for extended periods); confusing regular and hidden divergence.
Strategy 07
Flag Patterns
The structural continuation pause after an impulsive move
M15 / H1 trigger Continuation Entry trigger
A flag forms when a sharp impulse — the flagpole — is followed by a tight, sloping consolidation against the direction of the move. The consolidation is where short-term profit-takers exit and new participants accumulate positions for the next leg. The breakout from that consolidation is the most structurally precise continuation trigger available, because it tells me both the direction (the prior impulse) and the exact level (the channel break) in a single pattern.
Market mechanic After an impulsive break, the desks that initiated the move are not yet finished. Their goal is to add to the position at a better price before the next leg. They allow weak counter-trend participants — profit-takers and contrarians — to absorb the immediate pressure and create the consolidation channel. Once that supply is exhausted, the desks resume their original direction, breaking out of the flag with the same conviction as the original impulse. The flag breakout is therefore the point at which the consolidation supply has been fully cleared.
Execution rules
  • The flagpole must be sharp and clean — at least 30 pips on majors, with no major retracement during the impulse itself.
  • The flag should be tight and orderly — typically 3 to 8 candles, sloping against the trend at 30–60 degrees. Wide or messy consolidations are not flags.
  • Entry on the candle close beyond the flag boundary, not the wick. Wick-based breakouts fail at a far higher rate.
  • Stop loss 5–10 pips beyond the opposite side of the flag. The tight stop is the whole point — flags consistently produce 1:3 or better.
  • Take profit at flagpole height projected from the breakout (the textbook measured move), or at the next AOI or structural high/low if closer.
Common mistakes I avoid: Trading flags without a clear flagpole (the prior impulse is the whole signal); entering on a wick break instead of a candle close (high false-break rate); over-trading wide consolidations as if they were flags (they are ranges, not continuations).
Before any entry
Non-negotiable pre-trade checklist
✓Higher-timeframe direction — H4 and D1 must agree with the trade direction. No exceptions, no counter-trend entries on majors.
✓Setup category named — which of the seven strategies is this? If I cannot name it explicitly, I do not take it.
✓Lower-timeframe confirmation — M15 or M5 must show a reaction, not just price arriving at the zone.
✓RSI alignment — divergence supports, hidden divergence supports, or RSI is at minimum not actively contradicting the trade.
✓Session timing — London or New York only. No Asian session entries on majors. No Friday afternoon entries.
✓News clearance — no high-impact economic release within 30 minutes either side of intended entry.
✓Minimum 1:2.5 R:R — if the nearest opposing structural level does not give at least 1:2.5, the asymmetry is not worth the risk.
✓Stop loss defined pre-entry — I never enter without knowing where I am wrong. Mental stops are not stops.
✓Partial plan committed — 30% at 1:1, 30% at 1:2, 40% runner. Stop moves to break-even at 1:1. Always.
The principle behind every rule
Every rule above exists because I broke it once and it cost me real capital. The strategies themselves are not the hard part — patterns can be taught and replicated. The hard part is the discipline to wait, to skip the close-but-not-quite trade, to take partials instead of holding for the moon, and to walk away after three losses instead of trying to win it back the same session. Consistency is not in the setups. It is in the patience to refuse the wrong ones.

Section 03
What Actually Moves Each Currency
Rate differentials matter, but they're not the whole story. Every economy depends on someone else for something — exports, energy, capital, or safe-haven demand. When those dependencies shift, currencies move before central banks even speak. Below: who relies on whom, and what to watch.
AUD Australia · The China Proxy
Australia exports iron ore (35% of total exports), coal, LNG, and copper — over a third of all exports go to China alone. The relationship: Chinese steel mills buy Australian iron ore to build property and infrastructure. When Chinese property developers default or PBoC tightens, mills cut orders, iron ore prices crash, and AUD follows within days.
Trade web: China (32% of exports) · Japan (15%) · South Korea (6%) · India (5%). Watch out for: Chinese Evergrande-style defaults, PBoC LPR cuts (stimulus = AUD up), Chinese steel rebar futures.
WatchChina Manufacturing PMI · Iron ore futures (Dalian) · PBoC LPR rate · Chinese property data · Caixin services PMI
JPY Japan · The Energy Importer
Japan imports ~90% of its energy — particularly crude oil and LNG from the Middle East (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar). When Strait of Hormuz tensions flare or Iran-Israel conflict escalates, oil prices spike → Japan must sell yen to buy more USD for crude → JPY weakens hard. The current Iran war is exactly why USD/JPY broke 159.
Energy dependence: 95% oil imported, 98% LNG imported. Saudi (40% of crude), UAE (25%), Kuwait (8%). Secondary driver: world's largest carry trade funder — when risk-off hits, leveraged yen shorts unwind violently and JPY rallies in days.
WatchBrent crude · Strait of Hormuz headlines · US 10-year yield · MoF intervention rhetoric · Japan trade balance
CAD Canada · The Oil Exporter, US Partner
Canada is the 4th-largest oil producer globally and the largest crude supplier to the US. WTI crude is the single biggest CAD driver — when oil rallies, USD/CAD falls. But here's the twist: 75% of all Canadian exports go to the US, so when Washington threatens tariffs on Canadian steel, aluminum, or autos, CAD weakens regardless of oil. The two forces pull in opposite directions.
Trade web: US (75% of exports) · China (4%) · UK (3%). Right now: Iran-war oil shock supporting CAD, but US tariff threats on Canadian autos under CUSMA review keep BoC trapped — can't cut while energy fuels inflation, can't hike while tariffs threaten growth.
WatchWTI crude · US-Canada tariff headlines · CUSMA review · US ISM Manufacturing · BoC speaker calendar
CHF Switzerland · The Safe Haven
CHF strengthens when the world gets scared. The Swiss banking system holds ~$2 trillion of foreign capital through private banking and family offices — during banking crises, equity sell-offs, or geopolitical shocks, those funds flow back home, bidding up CHF fast. Switzerland's surplus current account (~7% of GDP) adds permanent buying pressure.
Trade web: EU (54% of exports) · US (16%) · China (6%) — exports are pharma (40%), watches, machinery. SNB intervention: Switzerland intervenes in FX more than any G10 central bank — they sell CHF for EUR when franc gets too strong (current bias to intervene above 0.92 EUR/CHF).
WatchVIX index · S&P 500 daily ranges · EU bank stress · SNB FX reserves data · EUR/CHF 0.92 level
NZD New Zealand · The Dairy Trade
NZ is a small economy with one dominant export: dairy. Fonterra alone moves roughly 30% of global dairy exports, and dairy products are ~20% of all NZ exports. The fortnightly Global Dairy Trade auction prints results every other Tuesday — NZD often moves 30-50 pips on the headline. Like AUD, NZD is also China-sensitive: China buys 28% of NZ exports, mostly dairy and meat.
Trade web: China (28%) · Australia (12%) · US (11%) · Japan (6%). Cross to watch: AUD/NZD trades the relative health of the China bloc — when iron ore beats dairy, AUD/NZD rises.
WatchGlobal Dairy Trade auction (fortnightly Tue) · China growth · AUD/NZD cross · NZ trade balance · Risk sentiment
EUR Eurozone · The German Engine, Russian Vulnerability
The EUR is really a German manufacturing currency. Germany is 30% of Eurozone GDP and dominates exports (cars to China, machinery worldwide). When German industrial production drops or BMW/Mercedes sales in China collapse, EUR follows. The bloc also imports ~60% of its energy — pre-2022 from Russian gas pipelines, now from Norway, US LNG, and Qatar, all priced in USD. Energy shocks hit EUR especially hard.
Internal fault lines: Italian and French sovereign bond spreads vs German Bunds = EU political risk gauge — when Italy/France yields blow out, EUR drops. Trade web: US (18% exports) · UK (13%) · China (10%) — China dependence makes Eurozone vulnerable to PBoC and US-China tariff wars too.
WatchGerman Manufacturing PMI · Dutch TTF gas prices · Italian-Bund spread · ECB speaker calendar · German auto export data
GBP UK · Services & Financial Sector
The UK is ~80% services — financial services, professional services, tourism. That makes UK inflation stickier than goods-driven economies (services prices fall slowly). The UK also runs a persistent current account deficit (~3% of GDP) — it imports more than it exports and relies on capital inflows. When global risk appetite drops, foreign investors pull money from London, and GBP falls hard.
Trade web: EU (42% of exports — biggest partner post-Brexit), US (22%), China (5%). Special factors: London financial sector beta — when global banks have good days, GBP firms. Gilt yields signal fiscal stress (2022 mini-budget crisis still fresh).
WatchUK Services CPI · UK Wage Growth · BoE MPC vote splits · 10-year Gilt yield · UK retail sales
USD USD · The Reserve Currency Everyone Else Needs
USD is the world's reserve currency — ~60% of global FX reserves, ~80% of trade finance, ~90% of FX market volume. Everyone needs dollars: emerging markets to service USD debt, oil importers to pay for crude, central banks for reserves. That structural demand means USD strengthens whenever (a) US rates are higher than peers, (b) global growth weakens (flight to safety), or (c) dollar liquidity tightens (Fed QT).
Special weight: NFP, US CPI, and Fed speeches move every major pair simultaneously — USD is the common denominator. Watch the DXY (USD vs EUR, JPY, GBP, CAD, CHF, SEK basket) as the single dollar dashboard.
WatchNFP · US CPI · DXY · US 2yr and 10yr yields · Fed speaker calendar · ISM Manufacturing & Services
XAU Gold · Real Yields, Fear & Central Bank Buying
Gold has no yield and no counterparty, so it competes directly with real (inflation-adjusted) US bond yields. When real yields fall, gold rises. It's also the ultimate fear asset — geopolitical shocks, banking crises, dollar debasement fears, all push it higher. The new tailwind: central banks (especially China, Russia, India, Turkey) buying gold to diversify away from USD reserves — over 1,000 tonnes/year of official sector demand since 2022.
Demand structure: Jewelry 50% (China + India dominant) · Investment 25% (ETFs, bars, coins) · Central banks 23% · Industrial 2%. Watch out for: ETF outflows after big rallies — when retail finally piles in via GLD, professionals often distribute.
WatchUS 10yr real yield (TIPS) · DXY · World Gold Council central bank data · GLD ETF flows · Geopolitical headlines
CNY China · Why It Matters Even If You Don't Trade It
You probably don't trade USD/CNY directly, but China moves AUD, NZD, EUR, JPY, and commodity currencies indirectly. China is the world's second-largest economy, biggest manufacturer, and biggest buyer of commodities. PBoC LPR rate cuts, property stimulus, or PMI prints set the tone for risk sentiment globally. When PBoC devalues the yuan (a recurring theme), it exports deflation worldwide — AUD/USD drops, copper drops, oil drops.
Why traders watch CNY: PBoC daily fix at 09:15 China time signals policy bias. CNY weakening = global deflation impulse. CNY strengthening = risk-on. Tariff war live wire: Trump-era tariffs still in place under the current US administration — any escalation hits commodity currencies first.
WatchPBoC daily fix · USD/CNH offshore · Caixin Manufacturing PMI · China retail sales · US tariff headlines
OIL Oil · The Currency Multiplier
Oil isn't a currency but it moves several at once. Higher oil = CAD strengthens, JPY weakens, EUR weakens, USD mixed (US is net oil exporter now). The current Iran-Israel conflict is the dominant macro driver in 2026 — every central bank is raising inflation forecasts because of crude. Strait of Hormuz carries 20% of global oil — any disruption headline moves all energy currencies in seconds.
Producer-consumer split: Exporters (CAD, NOK, RUB, MXN) win on oil up. Importers (JPY, EUR, INR, KRW) lose. OPEC+ matters: Saudi-Russia production cuts have set the floor since 2023.
WatchBrent crude · WTI crude · OPEC+ meetings · Middle East headlines · US EIA crude inventories (weekly)
